From Posters to Planets..

Author
Eljos
Published
June 11, 2025

I don’t remember the first time I sang — but I do remember that I never stopped. Music, in many ways, was my first universe. Long before I ever heard of biology or touched a computer keyboard, I was already feeling rhythms and making melodies. I would sing in school and church, drum on tabletops and tin cans, and over time, I found myself drawn to instruments — starting with drums, and eventually the keyboard, where melody and rhythm met. I didn’t study music formally — I just followed sound like a river, letting it lead me.

Then came a different kind of creation — not through instruments, but through hands-on building. From around grade 3 to 6, I was that kid who’d collect broken toys and old wires, not to throw away, but to build something. I had a bucket full of gears, motors, wires, tiny screws — and an imagination running wild. I turned old RC cars into homemade bots. I made mini drills from broken motor fans. I even built a vibration-based glove for the visually impaired, powered by Arduino. I didn’t call it "STEM" or "robotics" back then — it was just curiosity with a purpose.

At the same time, my eyes were slowly opening to the world of design — not by books or courses, but by simply watching my father work. He was a graphic designer, and I would quietly observe him navigating CorelDRAW, shaping visuals with intention. It wasn’t long before I began experimenting with design tools myself. Posters, school presentations, brochures, ID cards, event logos — anything that needed a visual touch, I was drawn to. Design felt like music for the eyes.

But it was one evening under the stars that shifted everything.

A friend once pointed to the moon and said, “Doesn’t that shadow look like Mary and child?” I looked — it kinda did. But something in me was more curious about the moon itself. How does it glow like that? What’s it really made of? Why does it pull the ocean? That night, I looked at the sky a little longer — and never quite stopped. Every time I stepped outside, my eyes instinctively searched for the moon and stars, each twinkle tugging at something deeper inside me.

That simple act of looking up led me to questions I never imagined asking. Is there life beyond Earth? What makes a planet livable? What if we’re not alone? I wasn’t just dreaming — I was searching. And space wasn’t just a mystery anymore. It was a calling.

But even as I gazed upward, I was still building things down here.

In 9th grade, during a vacation, I stumbled upon video editing. I started experimenting with After Effects and 3D motion graphics. Hours flew by as I tweaked frames and played with timelines. Then I discovered coding. It started with HTML and CSS, turned into full-fledged JavaScript obsession, and soon expanded into React.js, React Native, Flutter, Python, and beyond. I was building websites, experimenting with apps, and even made a Marks Analysis system for my brother’s school. I wasn’t just coding — I was crafting experiences.

Chapter of Life?

Then came 2019. Then came the pandemic.

While the world paused, we created.

With just a modest PC, a small mic, a headset, and a keyboard, my brother and I built our first home studio. He’d play the keyboard, and I’d handle everything else — vocals, drum programming, sound mixing, and music production. We tried every DAW we could lay our hands on — from Audacity to Mixcraft, Reaper to Cubase. We failed, tried again, messed up, fixed it, and over time, made magic. It wasn’t perfect, but it was ours. It was music from a room full of dreams.

Through all these years, one thing remained constant — I never stuck to one lane. Whether it was making music, designing posters, building machines, writing code, or staring at the stars — I was always led by curiosity. I didn’t have a plan, but I had passion. I didn’t have resources, but I had resourcefulness. I didn’t know where it would take me, but I knew I was going to enjoy the ride.

And now, by the grace of God, I stand at the beginning of a new chapter — as an incoming student of Integrated BS-MS in Biotechnology.

Some might see this as a pivot, but for me, it’s the perfect continuation. Biotechnology is about understanding life — decoding it, harnessing it, transforming it. It’s the science of what is, and the hope of what could be. And maybe, just maybe, it’s the bridge between Earth and the stars. The beginning of discovering life not just here, but out there.

So, that’s my story — or at least the first few verses of it.
I started with rhythm. Then I built with wires.
Then I drew with pixels. Then I coded with logic.
And now, I’m choosing life — exploring it at the molecular level, and dreaming of it across the galaxy.
Maybe everything really is connected.
And maybe that’s the point.

“Just a moment is enough for a spark of curiosity to enter a small mind and make it broader.” ~ Eljos
